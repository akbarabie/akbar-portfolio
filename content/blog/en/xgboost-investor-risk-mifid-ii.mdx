Most tutorials frame model selection as a leaderboard problem: run five algorithms, pick the one with the highest F1-score, ship it. That approach falls apart the moment your model's mistakes have regulatory consequences.

That's exactly the constraint I was working under while building a **4-class investor risk classifier** — Conservative, Income, Balanced, Aggressive — to support MiFID II-driven suitability assessments for Relationship Manager and Compliance teams, trained on 21,000+ investor records.

## The real objective wasn't accuracy

Under MiFID II, a Relationship Manager who recommends an aggressive product to a conservative investor isn't just wrong — they've created regulatory and reputational risk. So the success metric for this project was never just "highest F1-Macro." It was:

- Strong overall classification performance, **and**
- **Zero** cases where a Conservative investor gets misclassified as Aggressive (or vice versa)

That second constraint quietly eliminates any model that's merely "good on average." A classifier that nails 95% of cases but occasionally confuses the two extremes is more dangerous here than one with slightly lower accuracy but a clean safety record on the extremes.

## Benchmarking honestly, not cherry-picking

Before touching any model, the preprocessing pipeline had to be leakage-safe: stratified train-test split, IQR-based outlier capping, and a `ColumnTransformer` combining `StandardScaler` with a hybrid One-Hot/Ordinal encoding scheme — chosen only after validating feature relevance with ANOVA F-tests and Chi-Square tests, and resolving multicollinearity through correlation-driven feature selection.

With the pipeline settled, I benchmarked five algorithms under identical conditions using 5-fold cross-validation:

1. K-Nearest Neighbors
2. Support Vector Machine
3. Decision Tree
4. Random Forest
5. XGBoost

XGBoost came out ahead — not by a landslide, but consistently across folds, and critically, without violating the zero-misclassification constraint on the extreme classes.

## Squeezing out the last bit of performance

Once XGBoost was selected as the baseline winner, I tuned it further with `RandomizedSearchCV` rather than an exhaustive grid search — with a 21k-row dataset and multiple hyperparameters to explore, randomized search gets you close to the optimum at a fraction of the compute cost:

```python
from sklearn.model_selection import RandomizedSearchCV
from xgboost import XGBClassifier

param_distributions = {
    "n_estimators": [200, 300, 400, 500],
    "max_depth": [3, 4, 5, 6],
    "learning_rate": [0.01, 0.05, 0.1],
    "subsample": [0.7, 0.8, 0.9, 1.0],
    "colsample_bytree": [0.7, 0.8, 0.9, 1.0],
}

search = RandomizedSearchCV(
    estimator=XGBClassifier(objective="multi:softprob", eval_metric="mlogloss"),
    param_distributions=param_distributions,
    n_iter=50,
    scoring="f1_macro",
    cv=5,
    n_jobs=-1,
    random_state=42,
)

search.fit(X_train, y_train)
```

This tuning pass improved F1-Macro by roughly **1.3 points** over the untuned baseline — a modest-sounding number, but meaningful when the untuned model was already competitive, and every fraction of a point comes from genuinely better decision boundaries rather than noise.

> The safety property — zero high-risk misclassifications — held throughout every iteration of tuning, not just in the final model. That was the actual acceptance criterion, not the leaderboard score.

## Shipping it somewhere reviewers can actually touch

A notebook with a good F1-score doesn't help a Compliance team evaluate whether they trust the model. So the final classifier was wrapped in a custom preprocessing transformer module for production-safe serialization, and deployed as an interactive Streamlit app on Hugging Face Spaces — letting Relationship Managers and Compliance stakeholders enter investor attributes and see the predicted risk class directly, without needing to read a single line of Python.

## The takeaway

If there's one thing worth carrying into the next classification project, it's this: **the metric you optimize for should come from the cost of being wrong, not from what's easiest to compute.** F1-Macro was necessary but not sufficient here — the real spec was "never confuse the two extremes," and every architectural decision, from feature encoding to hyperparameter search, was made in service of that constraint first.
